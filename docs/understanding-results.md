# Understanding Your Results

This page explains what every classification, number, and badge in your report means — and, importantly, what it does not mean.

---

## The most important thing to understand first

**A genetic variant is not a diagnosis.**

Every finding in your report is a statistical association between a genetic variant and a health outcome, drawn from population-level studies. These studies show that people who carry a particular allele are, on average, more or less likely to develop a condition compared to people who don't carry it.

This does not mean:
- You will develop the condition
- You are currently affected by the condition
- A "high risk" finding is cause for alarm

It means the association exists in research populations and is worth being informed about so you can make intentional choices.

Many of the world's healthiest, longest-lived people carry "high risk" variants. Lifestyle, environment, other genes, and chance all interact with genetic predisposition in ways that cannot be predicted for any individual.

---

## Risk levels

### Homozygous Risk — High Risk (red)

You carry **two copies** of the risk allele — one inherited from each parent. This is the highest-risk genotype for that variant.

Statistically, this means the association found in research populations applies most strongly to your genotype. It does not mean the outcome is inevitable.

Example: A person with two copies of the APOE ε4 allele has roughly 8–12× the population average risk of developing Alzheimer's disease. Roughly 30–50% of people with this genotype develop Alzheimer's. That means 50–70% do not.

### Heterozygous — Moderate (amber)

You carry **one copy** of the risk allele. The statistical association is real but weaker than the homozygous case — often described as roughly half the effect size.

### Protective (green)

Your genotype is associated with a **reduced risk** or a favorable physiological trait. These are genuinely good findings — variants where the research suggests your genes are working in your favor.

### Normal (green, hidden by default)

You carry **no copies** of the risk allele for this variant. No action needed. These findings are hidden by default to keep the report focused on what matters.

## Confidence levels

Each finding card shows a confidence rating: `high`, `moderate`, or `low`. This reflects the strength of the underlying evidence:

| Level | What it means |
|---|---|
| **High** | Multiple large, well-replicated studies. The association is considered established in the scientific literature. |
| **Moderate** | Replicated in multiple studies but with some heterogeneity, or based on fewer samples. |
| **Low** | Preliminary evidence. The association has been reported but not yet robustly replicated. |

For findings from downloaded databases (GWAS Catalog, ClinVar, PharmGKB), the confidence is derived from p-values, evidence levels, and clinical significance classifications.

## Understanding the action plan

Every recommendation in the Action Plan is generated from your actual variants — not generic wellness advice.

**How a recommendation gets generated:**

1. A variant in your file matches a database entry
2. Your genotype is classified as Moderate or High Risk for that variant
3. The gene and trait keywords trigger specific recommendation logic
4. If the same recommendation would be generated by two different variants, it only appears once (deduplicated)

**What the gene tag means:**

Each bullet in the Action Plan shows a small gene tag (e.g., `MTHFR`, `APOE`). This is the specific variant responsible for that recommendation. If you want to understand why a recommendation is there, find that gene in the relevant category tab and read its finding card.

**"General" recommendations:**

Some bullets show `General` instead of a gene name. These are universal recommendations that apply regardless of specific variants — for example, sleep 7–9 hours, resistance train 3×/week. They are included because the evidence base for them is strong enough to apply universally.

## The matched variants count

In the Overview and Welcome modal, you will see a count like **"47 variants matched."**

This means 47 rsIDs in your DNA file were found in the analysis database. The count includes all genotypes — high risk, moderate, protective, and normal. Not all matched variants will appear in the report tabs — only those where the interpretation produced an actionable finding (non-normal status) or where the variant is explicitly tracked.

A low matched count (under 20) may indicate:
- Your provider uses a different genotyping array that covers different positions
- The file format was not detected correctly (check [Supported File Formats](file-formats.md))
- Your file is from a less common provider

A typical analysis against the built-in 357-variant database will match 150–300 variants, depending on provider. With GWAS Catalog or ClinVar installed, matched counts can reach into the thousands.

## The pharmacogenomics (drug response) section

The Drug Response tab deserves special attention because it has immediate practical implications.

Pharmacogenomic variants affect how your body metabolizes, responds to, or tolerates specific medications. Common examples:

- **CYP2C19** — affects how you process clopidogrel (Plavix), certain antidepressants, and proton pump inhibitors
- **SLCO1B1** — affects statin (cholesterol medication) transport; certain variants increase myopathy risk
- **VKORC1 / CYP2C9** — affects warfarin dosing sensitivity
- **CYP2D6** — affects a wide range of common medications including codeine, tamoxifen, and many antidepressants

**What to do with this information:**

1. Print or save your Doctor Summary
2. Bring it to appointments with your prescriber and pharmacist
3. Ask whether any medications you currently take or may take are affected by the variants listed
4. Do not stop, start, or change any medication based on this report alone

These are statistical risk signals, not clinical genetic tests. A formal pharmacogenomics panel from a certified laboratory provides legally actionable clinical guidance.

## What the report does not tell you

- **Current health status** — genetic variants are predispositions, not diagnoses. Blood tests, imaging, and clinical examination are how you determine current health status.
- **Certainty of outcomes** — no genetic test can tell you what will happen, only what statistical tendencies exist.
- **The full picture** — consumer genotyping arrays cover ~700K of ~3 billion base pairs. Important variants may exist in regions not covered by your provider's array.
- **Epigenetic state** — how your genes are actually being expressed right now is shaped by lifestyle, stress, environment, and age in ways that this report cannot capture.
- **Family history and environmental factors** — these remain among the strongest predictors of many conditions and are not reflected here.

## A note on disease risk findings

The Disease Risk category includes findings about Alzheimer's, cancer predispositions, hereditary conditions, and similar serious topics. These findings are evidence-based and worth knowing — but they carry significant psychological weight and should ideally be reviewed with a healthcare provider or genetic counselor if they are distressing.

The **⚠️ Risk** toggle in the toolbar and the **Hide Disease Risk Findings** option on the welcome screen exist for exactly this reason. You can choose to review those findings on your own terms.

If you carry APOE ε4 or a BRCA variant and want professional guidance on what it means for you specifically, genetic counseling is available through most major hospital systems and can often be accessed through your primary care physician.
